Saturday Morning at Wild Oats

Twenty-five years of bringing the Garden Route together, one breakfast at a time

It is just after seven on a winter Saturday morning, and the mist is still lifting off the Swartvlei lagoon. Already, the car park at Wild Oats is filling. Locals arrive on bicycles, dogs trotting alongside. The smell of woodsmoke and fresh coffee drifts through the trees. This is how the Garden Route has started its weekends for over a quarter of a century.

The Wild Oats Community Farmers' Market opened on 31 July 1999, after a night of torrential winter rain. Rose Brettell and Susan Garner, better known locally as "Rosie Oats" and "Suzy Wild," had spent two years planning and preparing: clearing dense alien vegetation from the lakeside site with chainsaw and determination, designing the distinctive barn-shaped stall structures from recycled hops-poles, and recruiting the first cohort of local producers.

By 10:30 that first morning, every last morsel of produce had sold out. The tiny village of Sedgefield had found its heartbeat.

The Philosophy

Wild Oats was never intended to be just another market. Rose and Susan had watched the spread of corporate retail across South Africa, the growing disconnect between consumers and the sources of their food. They believed something important was being lost: the knowledge of where food comes from, who grows it, how it reaches the table. A farmers' market, they reasoned, could restore that connection.

"Really authentic country markets offer a healthy community-building opportunity that stands in sharp contrast to the corporate shopping experiences that disengage shoppers from their local context, from seasonal produce, and from producers themselves."
— Rose Brettell & Susan Garner, Founders

The rules they established reflected this philosophy. Only local producers. Only food (no craft, despite the temptation of additional revenue). Producers must sell their own goods, ensuring customers meet the people who grew, raised, or baked what they are buying. Prices below average retail, to attract customers from across the region. Careful management of supply to minimise waste.

These principles have held firm through more than 1,300 Saturday markets. In all that time, only one has ever been cancelled, due to the severe floods of November 2007. Come rain or shine, Wild Oats opens.

A Morning at the Market

To experience Wild Oats properly, arrive early. By 7:30 in summer (8:00 in winter), the keenest shoppers are already moving between stalls, coffee in hand, filling baskets with the week's provisions. The regulars know the rhythm: which stall has the best sourdough, where to find the eggs laid yesterday, whose biltong is worth queuing for.

The market unfolds beneath a canopy of Australian myrtle trees, the Swartvlei lagoon glinting through the foliage. Over sixty vendors set up each week, their offerings a reflection of the Garden Route's agricultural abundance: free-range meats from local farms, vegetables still dusty from the field, honey harvested from hives in the Outeniqua foothills, cheeses aged in local cellars.

What You'll Find

  • Free-range beef, lamb, pork and chicken
  • Fresh vegetables and organic produce
  • Artisan breads and pastries
  • Farmhouse cheeses and dairy
  • Cured meats and biltong
  • Local honey and preserves
  • Fresh-cut flowers and indigenous plants
  • Homemade cakes and confectionery
  • International cuisines: German, Italian, Greek, Indian
  • Coffee, fresh juices, and smoothies

But Wild Oats is as much about breakfast as it is about shopping. The eastern edge of the market is given over to food stalls, where producers serve hot breakfasts cooked over coals: boerewors rolls, shakshuka, vegetarian wraps, German bratwurst. Families claim tables early, spreading out with newspapers and steaming plates while children run between the stalls.

At the entrance, the Eden Minstrels provide the soundtrack: a local band whose music has greeted visitors every Saturday for years. Their rhythms drift across the market, mixing with the chatter of neighbours catching up, the bark of dogs greeting each other, the calls of vendors announcing that the melktert is fresh from the oven.

More Than a Market

Wild Oats has always understood itself as something larger than a commercial enterprise. As a founding member of the Sedgefield Island Conservancy, the market has championed environmental awareness from the beginning, educating customers about food miles, carbon footprints, and the value of eating locally and seasonally.

The economic impact ripples outward. For small producers who might otherwise struggle to reach customers, the market provides a reliable, well-promoted outlet. Money spent here stays local, circulating through the community rather than disappearing into corporate supply chains. Young farmers have launched careers at Wild Oats; cottage industries have grown into established businesses.

But perhaps most importantly, Wild Oats has become a place where the Garden Route comes together. In a region of scattered towns and diverse communities, the market is neutral ground: a weekly gathering where permanent residents meet holiday visitors, where farmers meet city transplants, where the various threads of coastal life weave together over coffee and conversation.

"I've been to markets all over the world. I can safely say that Wild Oats is the best one I have ever been to. The vibes, variety, people and even the smells make it seem magical."
— Visitor review

The Saturday Circuit

Wild Oats is the anchor of what has become a full Saturday morning ritual for many visitors. Adjacent to the market, the Mosaic Market offers arts, crafts, and more food stalls, while the Scarab Craft Market completes the trio. It is entirely possible to arrive at 7:30 and not leave until well into the afternoon, having eaten two breakfasts, bought a week's worth of groceries, acquired several items you did not know you needed, and caught up with half of Sedgefield.

The market is dog-friendly, with designated areas for those with pets and treats available for four-legged visitors. Most stalls accept cards, though cash sometimes earns a small discount. Parking fills quickly during peak season, so locals recommend arriving early or cycling if you are staying nearby.

A Living Tradition

Twenty-five years on, Rose and Susan's vision has become woven into the fabric of Garden Route life. The market has won multiple awards, drawn international attention, and inspired similar initiatives elsewhere. But its real achievement is more modest: it has simply kept going, Saturday after Saturday, season after season, providing a place where community happens naturally.

There is something hopeful about Wild Oats. In an age of algorithmic feeds and contactless delivery, it insists on the old-fashioned virtues of showing up, meeting people, and buying food from the hands that grew it. The producers know their customers by name. The customers know where their food comes from. The whole transaction is human-scaled, tangible, real.

This Saturday, as every Saturday, the market will open at dawn. The coffee will be brewing, the coals warming, the vendors setting out their wares. The Garden Route will gather, as it has for a quarter of a century, under the trees by the lagoon. Come early. Bring a basket. Stay for breakfast.

Getting There

Wild Oats is located 1.5km west of Sedgefield town centre, at the Sedgefield Island turnoff next to the Engen petrol station. Follow the N2 from either George (20 minutes) or Knysna (15 minutes). The market is well signposted with green and white banners. Parking is available on site but fills quickly during peak season. Do not park on the N2 as police regularly issue fines.
