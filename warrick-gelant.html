Knysna's World Cup Winner

From Hornlee to Springbok glory—the remarkable journey of Warrick Gelant.

By Garden Route Living

When South Africa lifted the Rugby World Cup in 2023, a piece of Knysna lifted with it. Warrick Gelant, the fleet-footed fullback, had completed a journey that began on the dusty fields of Hornlee.

Hornlee is a township on the outskirts of Knysna, where rugby is played on fields without grass and boots are often hand-me-downs. It's not the obvious starting point for a World Cup winner. But Gelant's story is precisely why sport matters—proof that talent, properly nurtured, can transcend circumstance.

The Early Years

Gelant's talent was evident early. Local coaches identified something special in the way he moved with the ball, the instinctive timing of his runs. He progressed through Knysna's rugby structures, eventually earning a place at Grey High School in Port Elizabeth—a traditional rugby powerhouse.

The transition wasn't easy. Leaving home, adapting to a new environment, competing against players from more privileged backgrounds. But Gelant's speed and game sense kept opening doors.

"Hornlee taught me hunger. You learn to fight for everything when nothing is given to you."

The Rise

From Grey to the Bulls, from the Bulls to the Stormers, and finally to Racing 92 in Paris—Gelant's career arc has been steadily upward. His selection for the Springboks, and inclusion in the World Cup squad, represented the culmination of years of work.

Coming Home

Gelant remains connected to Knysna. When he returns, he visits the fields where he first played, speaks to young players coming up the same path. His success has become the community's success—proof of what's possible.

In a region known for natural beauty, Warrick Gelant represents a different kind of Garden Route treasure: homegrown talent that reached the pinnacle of world rugby without forgetting where it all began.
