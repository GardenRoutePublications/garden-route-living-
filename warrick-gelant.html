Knysna's World Cup Winner: The Warrick Gelant Story

From the streets of Hornlee to Springbok glory

When Warrick Gelant was nine years old, playing rugby on the dusty fields of Hornlee, he could not have imagined that one day he would hold a Rugby World Cup winner's medal. But in November 2019, that is exactly what happened. And when he returned home to Knysna, the whole town came out to welcome their hero.

Warrick Wayne Gelant was born on 20 May 1995 in Knysna, the coastal town at the heart of the Garden Route that has given the world oysters, indigenous forests, and now, a Springbok star. He grew up in Hornlee, a community on the edge of town where sport was not just recreation but a way out.

As a child, Gelant played everything: cricket, football, athletics. Rugby was actually almost the last sport he tried. But when he started, aged nine, something clicked. The natural balance, the ability to step off both feet, the explosive pace: these gifts, combined with hours of street games with friends, would eventually carry him to the pinnacle of the sport.

"Growing up in Knysna, I played a lot of different sports and we would always be having games in the street. I really wanted to represent my province in one of them."
— Warrick Gelant

A Talent Spotted

Gelant attended Hornlee Primary School, where coaches Frank Borchards and Neil Weber recognised something special. In 2008, the year he finished primary school, he was selected to represent South Western Districts (SWD) at the Under-13 Craven Week tournament. It was the beginning of a remarkable rise.

That performance caught the attention of Hoërskool Outeniqua in George, one of South Africa's rugby powerhouses. Through his coaches' efforts, Gelant secured a bursary to attend the school, leaving Knysna for the first time to pursue his dream. It was a 45-minute drive from home, but a world away from the streets of Hornlee.

At Outeniqua, known affectionately as "the Kwaggas," Gelant flourished. The school consistently ranks among South Africa's top junior rugby programmes, and Gelant quickly became one of its brightest stars. He represented SWD at the 2012 Under-18 Craven Week despite still being in the Under-17 age group, and was named to the South African Schools team, playing against France, Wales, and England.

The Professional Journey

After finishing school in 2013, Gelant moved to Pretoria to join the Blue Bulls. It was a bold step for a young man from a small Garden Route town, but Gelant had ambition to match his talent. He worked his way through the ranks: Varsity Cup with Tuks, Junior World Championships with the South Africa Under-20s, and a memorable stint with the Blitzboks sevens team.

His performances earned him selection for the 2019 Rugby World Cup squad. In Japan, Gelant made his World Cup debut against Namibia, scoring a try, and crossed again against Canada. Then, on 2 November 2019 in Yokohama, the Springboks demolished England 32-12 to claim their third World Cup title. Warrick Gelant, the boy from Hornlee, was a world champion.

The Homecoming

Two weeks later, Knysna painted itself green and gold. Thousands lined Clyde Street as Gelant, tears in his eyes, made his way through his hometown in an open-top parade. Children chanted his name. Old neighbours rushed forward to shake his hand. The whole Garden Route celebrated.

"With all the fame that Warrick achieved, he still remains the same young kid that was playing in the streets of Hornlee. Every young kid running around in the streets of Hornlee can aspire to be like him."
— Ricky van Aswegan, Hornlee community leader and former teacher

What struck everyone who knew him was how unchanged he remained. When Gelant comes home to Knysna, he still stays at his parents' house in Hornlee. The fame, the money, the glamour of professional rugby: none of it has altered his connection to the community that raised him.

Coming Home to the Cape

In 2020, Gelant made a move that felt like destiny: he signed for the Stormers, bringing him back to the Western Cape. It was, he said, a "childhood dream" to play for the team he had watched growing up. But first, he had to overcome a significant challenge. An ACL injury, followed by double knee surgery, threatened to derail his career.

True to character, Gelant fought back. His return to form was remarkable. In October 2021, he was voted URC Player of the Month after a series of dazzling performances. And in 2022, he was instrumental as the Stormers claimed the inaugural United Rugby Championship title, a historic moment for Cape rugby.

Coach John Dobson, watching Gelant dismantle defences with his trademark sidesteps and offloads, compared him to cricket legend Graeme Pollock: "I remember people saying about Graeme Pollock that he just had so much time, and Warrick just looks like he has time."

What He Means to Knysna

Warrick Gelant's story resonates far beyond rugby statistics. In a region where opportunities can be scarce, he represents what is possible. Every child kicking a ball on the fields of Hornlee can look at Gelant and see a pathway. Not just to professional sport, but to a life beyond the circumstances of birth.

His humility, his connection to home, his refusal to forget where he came from: these qualities have made him more than just a sports star to the Garden Route. He is proof that you can reach the top of the world and still remember the streets where you learned to play.
